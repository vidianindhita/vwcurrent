Two Days in Sequoia, Not One Drop of Gas

  • Ralph Allora
  • June 16, 2021
  • 10 min read

In Fresno's historic district, there's a vintage service station that dates to 1926. It's the oldest such facility still operating in California, although these days it doesn't offer gas; it's an auto repair place. It maintains some ultra-cool antique gas pumps outside, though, and I'm a sucker for those.

So when I spotted them from the car, I immediately pulled over. My wife waited patiently as I stepped out to snap some photos. I jumped back in the car and away we went.

The irony of that stop was not lost on me. Just 15 minutes earlier, we'd been recharging our new electric vehicle, a 2021 Volkswagen ID.4 Pro. During a 500-mile round trip from our home in the Bay Area to a hiking weekend in Sequoia and Kings Canyon National Parks, these gas pumps would be the only ones we'd see up close.

This trip marked one month since we'd taken delivery of the ID.4 Pro—our first electric vehicle—and given up fossil fuels for good. Prior to the ID.4 we had owned a Lexus RX 350. It was nice enough; it had gotten us through multiple family road trips and it saw our daughter off to college last year. Yet we’d been experiencing an increasing sense of discomfort about driving an internal-combustion-engine SUV.

We consider ourselves eco-conscious and do all the right things: composting and recycling, sourcing our food locally, buying Energy Star appliances, you name it. That's why we finally had a reckoning about what we wanted in our garage. Whenever we'd take one of our many ski or hiking trips, we'd find ourselves standing amid some of the most beautiful and pristine natural treasures on Earth, knowing we'd arrived there in a carbon-spewing machine that was at least partially responsible for making the world a less habitable place.

When we saw that Volkswagen was introducing its first line of electric vehicles, and that the ID.4 would be produced in a net-carbon-neutral factory, we knew it was time to make the reservation. It didn't hurt that the lease deal included three years of free charging at Electrify America stations.

Upon picking up the ID.4, we felt as if a weight had been lifted by finally kicking the gas habit. The feeling was matched only by the pleasant surprise of getting all the perks we had come to expect in a nicely appointed SUV: plenty of interior room and cargo space, a full complement of active safety features, an easy-to-use infotainment screen, to name a few. We bought a ski rack for our future winter adventures, installed an Electrify America Level 2 home charger in our garage, and just like that, a new era had begun in our house.

"Upon picking up the ID.4, we felt as if a weight had been lifted by finally kicking the gas habit."

On the morning of our trip, we folded down the ID.4’s rear seats to fit our hefty day packs, overnight bags, and other gear; the cargo area accepted it all with room to spare. With the car fully charged, we rolled out.

In our first month of driving the ID.4, we hadn’t come close to exhausting its 250 miles of range in any given day—recharging it overnight was simple. We knew the ID.4’s range could even get us all the way from the Bay Area to Sequoia. But given the remoteness of our destination, we stopped along the way at a charging station, which we located via the Electrify America app, at a Walmart north of Fresno. A 350kW fast charger got us back to nearly full juice while we grabbed takeout breakfast sandwiches from a cafe across the street.

Vehicle and occupants thus replenished, we hit the road. Aside from the aforementioned photo session, we rode uninterrupted to our hiking spot for the day, the Alta Peak Trail. The trailhead is around a two-hour drive east of Fresno, reached by taking Route 180 East to Route 198 West.

The drive was smooth, and I mean it literally. Over years of car ownership, I'd become accustomed to the lurch of an automatic upshift whenever I hit the gas. A few weeks of merging onto highways in the ID.4 made me forget all that. There's a seamless and virtually instant connection between pressure on the accelerator and the rapidly changing numbers on the speedometer. During our turns behind the wheel on this trip, neither my wife nor I really stomped on it—hi there, California Highway Patrol—but it's nice to know that we could.

Hiking Alta Peak is a challenge. It's an approximately 15-mile round-trip route, changing 4,500 feet in elevation along the way. The steepness of the final couple miles will test you, so don't skip leg day at the gym. We had filled our packs with plenty of water, along with sandwiches and snacks, for a hike that would take us over eight hours.

We began along the Lakes Trail, then veered off at a fork to the Alta Peak Trail. We marveled at the surrounding thick forest of ancient sequoias and sublime views of the Great Western Divide and Castle Rock. In the final stretch, we pushed ourselves to climb an increasingly steep and rocky incline. Our efforts were worth it. At the 11,200-foot summit, we stood silently in awe of the vista of Sequoia National Park, a breathtaking expanse of high-country beauty. Here were stunning views of glacial peaks, massive valleys, and the glittering Pear Lake. Off in the distance was Mt. Whitney, the tallest peak in the continental U.S. It was a humbling reminder that there's much to appreciate in this world, far bigger than ourselves.

"Standing at the summit was a humbling reminder that there's much to appreciate in this world, far bigger than ourselves."

We returned to the trailhead in the early evening. Our bodies aching, we melted into the cocoon-like environment of the ID.4 and wound our way northeast on Route 198 East for around an hour before arriving for dinner at the Pinehurst Lodge in Miramonte.

In the parking lot, a couple approached us, pointed to the ID.4 and asked, "Excuse, me, what is that?" While I can't recall this ever happening with our previous SUVs, we had now come to expect it. The ID.4 doesn't look much like anything else I've seen on the road. It's got more character than a typical electric car, and in my opinion is much sleeker and more attractive than your average SUV. I'm glad we opted for the illuminated front light bar and VW logo, because when people see it, it helps settle the question of what it is.

After a meal of hearty pub food, we headed 11 miles northeast to our accommodations for the evening, the John Muir Lodge. The lodge is a fittingly rustic, stone-and-timber tribute to its namesake, the celebrated naturalist John Muir. We found open-beam ceilings and a stone fireplace with a salvaged redwood mantel in the lobby area, and custom wood furniture in our quiet and relaxing guest room.

"A couple approached us, pointed to the ID.4 and asked, 'Excuse, me, what is that?' I can't recall this ever happening with our previous SUVs."

Post-breakfast the next morning, we checked out. Inspired by the painting in the lobby depicting Muir as he surveyed the wild spaces he loved so much, we set off towards our second and final hike of the weekend. Given the rigors of yesterday's trek, we decided to take it a lot easier with this one.

Our destination was Buena Vista Trail, accessible by heading south from the lodge and turning onto Route 198. It's a two-mile, relatively laid-back hike to the top of Buena Vista Peak. Along the path, we admired the surrounding carpet of colorful wildflowers and views of massive glacial boulders. At the 7,600-foot-high summit, we got another worthwhile payoff: spectacular, sweeping views of the granite top of 8,200-foot-high Big Baldy Ridge, along with distant snowy peaks, the dramatically steep walls of Kings Canyon, a phalanx of majestically towering sequoias, and more.

The hike back to the trailhead was a breeze: one mile, all downhill. We left Sequoia and drove for about two and a half hours before making one final charging stop. We had a late lunch while we waited, and continued home. My wife was behind the wheel and singing along to ‘90s pop—she tends to win the battle of the road trip playlists—when she decided to mute the audio on Apple CarPlay.

There was no mechanical braying from an exhaust system. Apart from the soft whirr of wind and rolling tires, there was only silence. It was the automotive equivalent of the blissful stillness we experienced while standing at the summits and gazing upon the unspoiled wilderness that we’d give anything to preserve.

I’m not sure why it took us so long to finally go electric. But now that we’ve done so with the ID.4, we’re not looking back. As far as gas pumps go, I’m sure I’ll find some good photo opportunities down the road.
